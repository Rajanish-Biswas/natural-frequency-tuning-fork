Understanding the Measurements — Standards Reference

Velocity RMS (mm/s) — ISO 10816-3
The overall vibration severity of the machine casing. ISO 10816-3 defines four zones for pumps >15 kW: Zone A (0–2.3 mm/s) = new/good condition; Zone B (2.3–4.5) = acceptable long-term; Zone C (4.5–7.1) = investigate; Zone D (>7.1) = danger, shut down. Velocity is the most widely accepted broadband severity indicator for rotating machinery.


Displacement pk-pk (µm) — ISO 7919-3
Peak-to-peak shaft displacement measured at the bearing housing. ISO 7919-3 sets limits for pumps based on running speed. At 1475 RPM, typical Zone A limit is ≈ 57 µm pk-pk. Displacement is most sensitive at low frequencies (shaft orbit / unbalance); at high frequencies it becomes very small and less meaningful.


Spike Energy (gSE) — SKF / IRD Mechanalysis
A high-frequency stress-wave measurement (typically 5–50 kHz) that detects impacting in bearings. Developed by IRD Mechanalysis and adopted by SKF. It is independent of running speed. Typical alarm levels depend on machine speed and bearing size, but as a general guide: <0.25 gSE = good; 0.25–1.0 = monitor; 1.0–4.0 = schedule inspection; >4.0 = bearing distress, plan replacement. PDE readings at 6.2 / 4.6 / 4.2 gSE indicate active bearing distress at the pump drive end.


Why velocity + displacement alone cannot tell you fault type
Broadband readings give you how much vibration — not why. Unbalance, misalignment, looseness, and resonance can all produce similar broadband velocity values. To identify fault type you need a spectrum analyser (FFT) which separates the signal into individual frequency components (1×, 2×, sub-harmonic, bearing defect frequencies, etc.). No calculation from broadband velocity + displacement alone can reliably substitute for FFT analysis.
